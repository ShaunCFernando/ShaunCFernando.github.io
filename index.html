Academics & Research

I am a high school junior at Pennsylvania Leadership Charter School, University Scholars Program(USP). By the end of my junior year, I will have completed 16 Advanced Placement (AP) classes. I intend to take three additional AP Classes during my 12th grade year. My GPA is roughly 4.6. I am passionate about physics and calculus. My goal is to pursue a Bachelor of Science degree at the intersection of biomedical and aerospace engineering. I have been conducting DNA, microbial and cancer research since 6th grade. The Walter Reed Research Center supported my early research. During the summer of 7th grade I conducted my research at Central Catholic High School's AP Biology Laboratory under the supervision of Mark Krotec. In 8th grade I worked as an independent researcher at home building my own Arduino-powered robot, "Potamoi" to test the quality of water in the Monangahela, Allegheny and Ohio rivers. Additionally, I built a mobile app, "myH2O" to display near-realtime water quality results. In 9th and 10th grade I conducted research at , Hillman Cancer Center Academy "YES" research program affiliated with University of Pittsburgh Medical Center (UPMC) under the mentorship of Dr. David Boone, the Director of the Department of Biomedical Informatics and CoSBBI. During the two year program I built machine learning (ML) models to predict various cancer biomarkers.

For the aforementioned research, I received multiple awards. In eighth grade, Broadcomm Masters selected me as one of the top 300 Broadcom Masters. Some of the highlights of my awards are below,

  • National Broadcom Coding with Committment award from Broadcom Masters : presentation
  • National Lemelson Early Inventor prize.
  • Over 10 awards at the Pittsburgh Regional Science and Engineering Fair (PRSEF) and the Pennsylvania Junior Academy of Science (PJAS) Conference. I won multiple category awards for in the Robotics and Engineering division at PRSEF.

One week in Summer 2025 Keystone Boys State Program

Pennsylvania American Legion Keystone Boys State is a unique week long summertime educational program that does not emphasize classroom lectures and textbook learning. Instead, it focuses on participation and personal experience in a model state, complete with governing bodies and elected public officials. It is designed to mirror the structure and operation of its respective state government. When I was analyzing the water quality of Pittsburgh’s rivers as part of my independent research, I discovered alarming levels of contamination due to industrial waste and other pollutants. Despite developing a detailed remediation plan, I found it difficult to engage directly with local or state representatives to share my findings or advocate for change. This experience made me realize the importance of understanding how government works—not in just diagnosing and solving problems, but having the civic knowledge and leadership skills to address them through policy. My goal in attending Boys State is to gain firsthand experience in government, learn how to collaborate with peers to propose and pass legislation, and develop the confidence and tools to lead initiatives that protect our environment. Specifically, I hope to one day introduce a bill or policy at the state level that mandates standardized water quality monitoring, public reporting, and community-based remediation programs—beginning with the rivers I’ve already studied. Boys State represents the critical next step in turning my scientific research into real, actionable civic impact.
